# Marketing Promotion A/B Testing

Determining which of three marketing promotions delivers the highest sales for a fast-food chain, using A/B testing and statistical hypothesis testing.

## Overview

A fast-food company planning to add new menu items needed to decide which promotional strategy to run. Three promotions were tested across store locations, and the goal was to identify the one that drives the highest return. This project aggregates the raw campaign data, validates that the test groups are comparable, runs pairwise t-tests, and backs the conclusion with segment-level exploration (week, market size, store age).

## Goal

Decide which marketing strategy works best by comparing the average sales generated by Promotion 1, Promotion 2, and Promotion 3.

- **H0:** There is no difference in average sales between promotions.
- **H1:** There is a difference in average sales between promotions.

## Dataset

Source table: `turing_data_analytics.wa_marketing_campaign` (BigQuery).

Key fields used:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| `location_id` | Store location identifier |
| `promotion` | Promotion variant (1, 2, or 3) |
| `sales_in_thousands` | Sales figure per record |
| `market_size` | Large / Medium / Small |
| `age_of_store` | Store age in years (bucketed into 0–3, 4–7, 8–12, 13+) |
| `week` | Campaign week (1–4) |

Data was aggregated by `location_id` and `promotion` so each location contributes one observation per promotion before testing.

### Group summary

| Promotion | Locations | Mean sales | Std. dev. |
|-----------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| 1 | 43 | 58.10 | 16.03 |
| 2 | 47 | 47.33 | 14.50 |
| 3 | 47 | 55.36 | 16.38 |

## Methodology

1. **Exploration** — Counted distinct promotions and locations to understand the dataset.
2. **Aggregation** — Rolled sales up to one value per location per promotion.
3. **Overview statistics** — Computed count, mean, and standard deviation per promotion.
4. **Validation** — Checked that groups were balanced and locations evenly distributed, so the pools are comparable.
5. **Hypothesis testing** — Ran pairwise two-sample t-tests.
6. **Supporting analysis** — Broke performance down by week, market size, and store age group.

A significance level of **α = 0.01 (99% confidence)** was used to account for multiple pairwise comparisons and reduce the risk of a Type I error.

## Results

| Comparison | p-value | vs α = 0.01 | Outcome | Verdict |
|------------|---------|-------------|---------|---------|
| Promotion 1 vs Promotion 2 | 0.00128 | < 0.01 | Reject H0 | Significant difference |
| Promotion 1 vs Promotion 3 | 0.42 | > 0.01 | Fail to reject H0 | No significant difference |
| Promotion 2 vs Promotion 3 | 0.0136 | ≥ 0.01 | Fail to reject H0 | No significant difference |

Supporting segment analysis showed Promotion 1 with the highest average sales across every market size, every week (1–4), and every store age group.

## Recommendation

**Promotion 1 is the recommended strategy.** It significantly outperforms Promotion 2 (p = 0.00128) and shows consistently higher average sales across all segments. No statistically significant difference was found between Promotion 1 and Promotion 3 at the 99% level, but Promotion 1 performed higher on average throughout.

## Limitations

| Comparison | n | Confidence interval | Power |
|------------|---|---------------------|-------|
| Promotion 1 vs Promotion 2 | 43 vs 47 | 2.51 to 19.03 | Sufficient for large effect |
| Promotion 1 vs Promotion 3 | 43 vs 47 | −6.26 to 11.74 | Small effect, low power |
| Promotion 2 vs Promotion 3 | 47 vs 47 | −16.42 to 0.36 | Insufficient at 99% level |

The stricter α = 0.01 minimizes false positives (Type I error) but raises the chance of missing a real difference (Type II error). The Promotion 1 vs Promotion 3 comparison has a small effect size and a wide confidence interval that includes zero, so no firm conclusion can be drawn there. Collecting more data or extending the experiment would increase statistical power for a definitive comparison.
